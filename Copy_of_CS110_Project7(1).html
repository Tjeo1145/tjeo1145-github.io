Exam anxiety seems to have some corretlation to exam score, while study hours per day was a bit less clear, but still showed correlation. The exam anxiety also does not seem to have much correlation with study hours per day. The historgram plot shows that the majority of people who got below an 80 had a exam anxiety score of 10, and number of people got below an 80 gradually increased as exam anxiety went up, with a final large jump at 10. It is also really interesting that 0 people gave an exam anxiety score of below 5. The scatterplot also confirms that people with less exam anxiety tend to do better. Study hours is all over the place, since people who scored 80 and above had a variety of study hours, from 0 to 12, though more people who studied less than 6 hours had scores under 70 and people who studied more than that. It's interesting that the extremes are all over the place, with 0 hours of study resulting in all exam scores and a score of 100 resulting from all hours of study, but fewer from people who studied over 11 hours. So people who studied the most were less likely to get a low score, but also less likely to get 100. Some of the limitations to this analysis could actually be the size of the sample, because it makes the scatterplot somewhat difficult to read. The data is also from all students across multiple classes, and I'm not sure if this is all data for the same exam or not, which could also change results, since some exams are harder, some require less hours of study, and some might be considered more of a priority for some students over others, as well as the difference in teachers giving exam prep. It may help to break up the data with more variables, like separating the students by major or class.
